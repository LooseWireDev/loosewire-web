GDPR has been in force since 2018, but many teams still record their meetings using cloud transcription tools without thinking through the compliance implications. If you're subject to GDPR — and if you have any EU-based employees, clients, or meeting participants, you likely are — it's worth understanding what's actually required.

This isn't legal advice. But here's a practical framework for thinking about meeting transcription and GDPR.

## What GDPR covers

GDPR applies to the processing of "personal data" — any information relating to an identified or identifiable natural person. Meeting recordings clearly contain personal data: voices, names, and the content of conversations about individuals or business matters involving people.

The key obligations for meeting transcription are:

**Lawful basis.** You need a legal reason to process the data. For internal meetings, "legitimate interests" is often the relevant basis. For client or external calls, explicit consent or contractual necessity may be more appropriate.

**Data minimisation.** You should collect only what's necessary. If you only need action items, recording and storing the full audio indefinitely may not be proportionate.

**Storage limitation.** Personal data shouldn't be kept longer than necessary. Indefinite cloud storage of meeting recordings may not comply.

**Data subject rights.** Anyone recorded in a meeting has rights: access, rectification, erasure. If their data is on a third-party platform, exercising these rights becomes complex.

**Third-party processors.** If you use a cloud transcription service, that service becomes a data processor under GDPR. You need a Data Processing Agreement (DPA) with them. Many transcription vendors offer DPAs, but they may not cover all data residency requirements.

## The problem with cloud transcription

When you use a tool like Otter.ai or Fireflies, your meeting audio is transmitted to and processed by a third-party service. Under GDPR:

- That service is a data processor
- You need a DPA in place
- You need to understand where their servers are located (data residency matters under GDPR's third-country transfer rules)
- You need to be able to respond to data subject access requests that might relate to that data
- You're responsible for their security posture as it relates to your data

This isn't impossible to manage, but it's non-trivial. And for many organisations, legal review of these vendor relationships is slow or simply doesn't happen.

## How local transcription changes the picture

When transcription happens entirely on your machine:

- **No third-party processor** for the audio — eliminating the DPA requirement for that stage
- **No cross-border transfer** of audio data — the recording stays in the country where the device is
- **Easier data deletion** — the files are on your machine, in a folder you control
- **Simpler data subject requests** — you know exactly where the data is

## Practical steps for compliance

**Inform participants.** Whether you're recording locally or in the cloud, participants should know. A brief mention at the start of a call is good practice and, in some jurisdictions, legally required.

**Define a retention policy.** Decide how long you'll keep meeting transcripts and enforce it. Local files are easier to delete on schedule than cloud-stored recordings.

**Review your vendor DPAs.** If you're using any cloud transcription tool, make sure you have a DPA in place and understand the data residency implications.

**Prefer text over audio.** If you can transcribe locally and delete the audio immediately, you reduce the personal data you're holding. A text transcript is meaningfully less sensitive than the original audio.
